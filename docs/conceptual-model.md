# Conceptual Model

RCRE starts from the idea that requirements should be understood as consumable information artifacts.

A requirement becomes consumable when the intended reader can move through a minimum reader-side consumption chain:

Role-specific questioning
        ↓
Meaning recovery
        ↓
Follow-up action

This chain leads to the three dimensions of reader-centricity.

## Role-Interrogativity

Role-Interrogativity concerns whether the requirement anticipates the entry questions of its intended readers.

Example reader questions include:
- What does this requirement mean for my role?
- What condition, behavior, or constraint must I consider?
- Which responsibility boundary is involved?
- Where should I look next if information is missing?

A requirement with high Role-Interrogativity does not need to answer every possible stakeholder question. It should answer or guide the questions that are relevant to the intended reader role.

## Interpretability

Interpretability concerns whether the intended reader can recover meaning without excessive search, guesswork, or dependence on tacit organizational knowledge.

Interpretability is broader than linguistic clarity. A sentence may be grammatically clear but still difficult to interpret if it depends on inaccessible references, undefined internal terms, scattered artifacts, or implicit assumptions.

A requirement with high Interpretability provides meaning at the point of use or gives accessible and relevant links to recover that meaning.

## Actionability

Actionability concerns whether the intended reader can determine what to do next.

Possible actions include:
- implementation,
- verification,
- acceptance,
- safety analysis,
- interface negotiation,
- review,
- field response,
- downstream refinement.

Actionability is related to verifiability, but it is broader. Verifiability asks whether compliance can be checked. Actionability asks whether the reader can derive an appropriate engineering action.
